# Can we predict evolution?

**Paper**

[Wortel, Meike T., Deepa Agashe, Susan F. Bailey, et al. “Towards Evolutionary Predictions: Current Promises and Challenges.” _Evolutionary Applications_ 16, no. 1 (2023): 3–21.](https://doi.org/10.1111/eva.13513).

**What is being predicted**

The review discusses when evolution is predictable. It provides many examples of applied approaches, focusing on predictions of how populations will evolve. In a few words "many factors influence the predictability of evolution. In a highly predictable scenario, the efficiency of selection is high relative to the stochasticity of genetic drift, mutation, recombination and unpredictable environmental changes. In such a situation, the fitness increase of the population and possibly the increase in population size, and key fitness-­related traits can be accurately predicted with **deterministic models**. "

Makes the point of going towards evolutionary control, not just prediction.

For similar discussion but focused on AMR from systems biology perspective see [Petrungaro_2021](Petrungaro_2021.md) [Charlebois_2023](Charlebois_2023.md)

**How**

- Experimental evolution
- Using the mutational and fitness landscape
- Metabolic and growth models
- Population genetic models
- Quantitative genetics and the breeder's equation 
- Epidemiological models (SIR models)
- Species distributions across space and environmental conditions
- Multi-scale evolutionary modelling
- Machine learning

**Limitations/open problems**
- The difference of language between  subfields. "Because researchers in different sub fields use different languages, it is not always obvious that they are, in essence, doing the same thing: predicting the future state of an evolving population. Predictions can be improved when re searchers can learn and be inspired by results from other subfields, but for this to happen, we need to use a common language "
- The need of mechanistic models of evolution "There will be a continued need to develop mechanistic models of evolution for various systems, for at least two fundamental reasons: (i) to extrapolate predictions to conditions other than those used to parameterize specific models and (ii) to further our understanding of how evolution works. While the increasing access to high-­resolution phenotype and genotype data make it tempting to include all these details in such models, more coarse-­grained mechanistic models may allow more powerful predictions.
