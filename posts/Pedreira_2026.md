# Can we predict ALE in mini-bioreactors?

### Paper

[Pedreira, Adrián, José A. Vázquez, and Míriam R García. “The Mini rEvolution: Application of Mini-Bioreactors in Adaptive Laboratory Evolution.” *Critical Reviews in Biotechnology*, 2026.](https://doi.org/10.1080/07388551.2025.2608012)

### What is being predicted

The review highlights how mini‑bioreactors allow researchers to predict evolutionary trajectories by offering precise control of selective pressures, environmental conditions, and population dynamics during ALE (adaptive laboratory evolution) experiments. With high parallelization and real‑time monitoring, these systems help anticipate how microbial populations will adapt—for example, by evolving improved nutrient utilization, tolerance to stressors, or resistance to antimicrobials. Predictions focus on phenotypic outcomes (growth rate increases, metabolic specialization, tolerance traits) under defined and repeatable selective landscapes.

For a similar review, but newer see [Charlebois_2023](Charlebois_2023.md)

### How

Mini‑bioreactors make prediction possible by enabling tightly controlled and reproducible environments, such as chemostats, turbidostats, and morbidostats, that maintain constant or dynamically regulated conditions over hundreds of generations. These systems incorporate automation, sensors (e.g., OD, dissolved oxygen), and programmable control algorithms, allowing continuous feedback‑based modulation of nutrient levels or stressor concentrations. Together, these features allow systematic experiments in which evolutionary responses can be quantitatively monitored, compared, and modeled. 

### Limitations/open problems

The review stresses that predicting ALE outcomes remains difficult because microbial evolution is influenced by biofilm formation, stochastic lineage behavior, and non‑ideal “steady states,” all of which can distort selection and measurement. Additionally, mini‑bioreactors can impose highly specific environmental constraints, meaning that evolved strains may display trade‑offs or reduced fitness in conditions different from the evolution environment. There is also a need for improved sensor integration, better biofilm‑prevention strategies, and deeper understanding of how complex or fluctuating environments affect long‑term evolutionary trajectories.
